Chinese Herbs

Chinese herbs have been used for centuries in Asian medicine practices. Formulations, comprised of over 5,700 substances (plants, animals, and minerals) in the form to teas, granules, patents, and tinctures have been passed down for generations. Today, there are 1,800 herbs in the Chinese pharmacopeia, classified according to their energetic qualities, functions and the different organs, meridians and disharmonies for which they address. Individual herbs are combined, using government official hierarchy such as emperor, minister, assistant, or servant, in order to enhance their actions and affects upon the body and create a unique synergistic formula. - The Emperor, Chief, or King herb is the ingredient that will have the greatest effect against the targeted pathology. - The Minister or Deputy, is the ingredient with two functions: 1) aiding the Emperor; and 2) serves as the main ingredient targeted toward coexisting pathologies. - The Assistant is the ingredient with three different functions: 1) Helpful Assistant: reinforces the Emperor or Minister, or potentially targets a less crucial pattern within the patholody. 2) Corrective Assistant: moderates or eliminates toxicity or harsh properties of the minister or emperor. 3) Opposing Assistant: used in complex disorders and has the opposite effect of the minister. - The Servant or Envoy, has two functions: 1) guides the actions of the formula to specific channel or areas of body; and 2) harmonizing and integrating the actions of the other ingredients. Additionally, to enhance the safe and effective use of the herbs, Chinese herbal medicine formulations are created according to six basic modes of herb-herb interactions; namely, reinforcement, potentiation, restraint, detoxification, counteraction, and toxicity. This combination increases the benefits of a particular formula, and minmizes any harmful side effects. Herbs are used in different quantities for different purposes to create the most effective and customized formula aimed at addressing the individual needs.
